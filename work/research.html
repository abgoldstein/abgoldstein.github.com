My research interests are focused mostly in Artificial Intelligence, Educational Data Mining, and Intelligent Tutoring Systems. Since arriving at WPI in 2009, I had a series of meetings with my two future advisors and almost immediately I started working on the idea that would eventually become my Master's Thesis. The tone of my academic writing is usually a bit more tempered and modest, but the concepts behind our work are genuinely fascinating, so please excuse me while I let myself be a little less formal and let some of my excitement shine through. Within the Intelligent Tutoring Systems community, research has emerged over the years that helps us to model students and their learning. We now are able to both detect and predict student affect, off task behavior, correctness, and much more. Back in 1995, two researchers at Carnegie Mellon named Al Corbett and John Anderson devised a method for tracking approximately how well a given student knows certain skills at any time. The technique is called Bayesian Knowledge Tracing and it is the foundation of a lot of work in the AIED/EDM/ITS field, including our own. However, what Bayesian Knowledge Tracing cannot tell you is when that knowledge was acquired. The work that I am doing with my advisors is focused on answering exactly that question. We are trying to capture the moment of learning. It's the instant that things "just click"; In Cognitive Psychology, the phenomenon is sometimes called "eureka moments". If we could know exactly when a student learns a skill, or will learn a skill, then the door is opened to all kinds of possibility; We can encourage students as we detect they're about to grasp a concept, study which questions or ordering of questions tends to lead to increased learning, and even find patterns and similarities between skills and concepts. There is a whole lot of potential and fascinating work that can be done following the ability to detect learning as it happens. During 2010, my advisors and I published papers that discussed our machine learned model called P(J), for probability of just learning, which attempts to pinpoint these learning moments. Now that we have P(J), my Master's thesis will be about measuring its utility. I have planned out with my advisers a randomized controlled experiment that will try to see if interventions based on changing values of P(J) have any effects on students. While we have shown that our model to predict P(J) is sufficiently accurate, this evaluation should tell us a lot about whether or not we really are capturing learning moments.
